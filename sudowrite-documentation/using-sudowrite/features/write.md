# **Write**

The Write feature is like the most advanced autocomplete ever. It works like this: Place your cursor at the end of some text, and then click Write. Sudowrite will continue it, picking up right where you left off.

To do this, Write looks back at the preceding text (up to 20,000 words) to gather context and pick up the thread. If your Story Bible is turned on (and filled out!) Write will also take into consideration your Style, Genre, Characters, Worldbuilding, and any linked Chapter Outline. Likewise, if you‚Äôve linked your chapters in sequence, Write can make use of Chapter Continuity and read prior chapters before writing.

Write works best with a _minimum_ of 20 words (not counting the document‚Äôs title), so ideally there should always be at least a couple of sentences before the cursor‚Ä¶ but the more words you provide, or the further into your document you use it, the better the results. Write can output anywhere from 50 to 2,000 words (you can tweak that number in your Write settings).

Click the üîΩ toggle to the right of Write to expand the Write menu. There you‚Äôll find three different versions of Write‚Äî ‚ú® Auto, ‚ÜîÔ∏è Guided, and ü•∏ Tone Shift‚Äîand the Write settings, which is where you‚Äôll control settings for these features.

Once you‚Äôve selected which Write mode you‚Äôd prefer, click the Write button within the text to use it. The suggestions will appear in the History column on the right. If you see a suggestion you like, click Insert to bring that prose into your document. This text will appear in **purple** until it's been edited by you. You‚Äôll get the best results by regularly editing the purple text to make it your own, and fully incorporate it into your work.

Think of Write \*\*\*\*as a writing partner. Write or paste a scene or chapter of your own into the Editor, and use Write to send the story off in different directions. You‚Äôll discover ideas and paths that will ultimately help enrich your story.

## ‚ú® Write - Auto

Write looks at what you've written and _continues_ it in the same style, voice, and format. Taking this into account, Write can help you with a number of creative tasks in your writing, including outlining, suggesting character traits, story themes, and much more. (These techniques work best in **Auto** mode.)

**OUTLINING**

Want help building out an outline for your story? Used creatively, Write can help! Start by giving Sudowrite a short summary of your story in several sentences. Then begin your outline and use Write after you've filled out the first few chapters or scenes.

Here's an example:

> \*Story summary: In a near-future San Francisco, an Indian-American doctor named Ruben performs genetic alterations for couples looking to have a child. Ruben enjoys his work but finds himself increasingly ethically conflicted as parents approach him with requests for modifications that he worries could be harmful to their children. He knows if he refuses, they'll just go get the job done by someone else. One day, he decides to forgo an agreed-upon genetic change and doesn't inform is clients. This decision haunts him and eventually extracts a high cost.
> 
> _OUTLINE_
> 
> _Scene 1: Ruben meets in his San Francisco office with a pair of new clients. They insinuate that they want their child to have genetic alterations that border on autism. Ruben tries to convince them otherwise but eventually agrees._
> 
> _Scene 2: Back home, Ruben watches his son doing his homework and thinks over the events of the day. He becomes angry as he considers his new clients' request. He decides not to honor it._
> 
> _Scene 3: Ruben is distracted all day, anxious that his subterfuge will be detected. But no one discovers it._
> 
> _Scene 4:_

By summarizing the story, then providing a scene-by-scene outline, we've given Write enough context to extrapolate ideas for where to go next. The consistent scene format tells Write what we're looking for, and by ending with "Scene 4: ", but not filling out that scene, we're strongly suggesting to Write that we'd like it to continue the outline we started.

As you fill out more of your outline, you can keep calling Write to continue it!

**EXPLORING CHARACTER BACKGROUND / THEMES**

Similar to the above example, we can exploit Write attempt to _continue_ what we've started to have it act as a brainstorming buddy for story and character ideas. Here's an example:

> Story summary: In a near-future San Francisco, an Indian-American doctor named Ruben performs genetic alterations for couples looking to have a child. Ruben enjoys his work but finds himself increasingly ethically conflicted as parents approach him with requests for modifications that he worries could be harmful to their children. He knows if he refuses, they'll just go get the job done by someone else. One day, he decides to forgo an agreed-upon genetic change and doesn't inform is clients. This decision haunts him and eventually extracts a high cost.
> 
> Some of the traits Ruben exhibits as a result of his Indian-American heritage and his training as a physician include:
> 
> \- Preference for self-sacrifice
> 
> \- Over-reliance on intellectual understanding
>

Again, we've started our example with a high-level background of a story. But the difference here is the 'prompt' we want Write to continue at the end. This time, instead of a story outline we want Write to continue, we‚Äôre using a prompt that explores the traits of our main character.

As before, the formatting is important. Here we're using a bulleted list to imply we want several short responses, and we end with a dash and a space before starting Write, strongly suggesting we want Write to continue the list where we left off. We‚Äôve ‚Äòprimed‚Äô our list with a couple of character traits, but we could change those or even leave them off completely and see how Write reacts.

What if instead of character traits, we wanted to explore story themes? We could start with a summary and end with a ‚Äòprompt‚Äô listing potential story themes. When we call on Write, the suggestions it gives us can help brainstorm possible themes for our story.

**GIVING CONTEXT TO WRITE**

It can be helpful to add a preamble to your text to give additional context to Write. This is useful if there aren't many words for Write to work with (< 500) or what you're writing is in a very unique or esoteric style that Sudowrite may not have seen before.

Examples:

> The following is a scene from a surrealistic prose poem that explores consciousness through dada-esque phrases: We are the sun. The sun is us. But the dodo is full on traveling to the moon, who is seen to be resisting. To resist is to accept being resisted, of course. Imagine a dodo, as big as the sun, traveling to the moon, but running into a turris.
> 
> The moon is not always full. Nor is the sun full always. The moon is the tongue of the sun.

In all of these examples, because our use of Write is ‚Äòoff-label‚Äô, we can expect some variability in the quality of the output. **Use at your own risk**, experiment with your prompt, and try running Write several times to see if you get better results. Also, when trying to get Write to focus in a certain document for these types of use cases, be sure you‚Äôve got Story Bible and/or Chapter Continuity turned off.

### Getting the most from Write‚Äôs Guided mode

In Guided mode, you can write a sentence or two to describe what should come next. Here‚Äôs what works best:

-   **Make it Believable** If your suggestion is too out of the blue, Sudowrite may have a hard time figuring out how to write it. For example, if you were writing a hardboiled detective story, and your guidance was for a magical genie to show up, Sudowrite may struggle to write that.
    
    -   Solution: Break your unbelievable idea into smaller, believable ideas. Maybe the detective finds a magic lamp, and then hears something coming from inside.
        
-   **Break up bigger ideas** Your suggestion should be doable in a couple paragraphs. For example, ‚ÄúThe two slowly fall in love and eventually get married, have kids, and start a bakery together‚Äù may be too much to cover in one go.
    
    -   Solution: Break long ideas into small chunks. For example, just start with ‚ÄúThe two agree to go on a date‚Äù, hit Write, then go again with the next part.
        

üí° **Try this!** Stop mid sentence

Guided mode will do a better job sticking to your story if you put the cursor mid-sentence before your click Write. This is because a period indicates to Sudowrite that a new idea should come next.

-   **Keep it simple** Simple sentences and repetition help Sudowrite understand what you want.
    
    -   Example: "Bill and Sophie get into a fight. After their fight, Sophie leaves." Because these instructions are short and clear, Sudowrite can easily understand that the fight is important, and that this is a sequence of **two separate actions**.

### Creativity

The Creativity slider dictates how strictly the Write feature will stick to what you‚Äôve already written. When the slider is at Least, this gives Sudowrite the least amount of flexibility to deviate from the prior context it read. It doesn‚Äôt mean the prose that Sudowrite comes up with is any less creative, instead the writing will just endeavor to match the arc of what is already in the document. Essentially, low creativity on this slider means the content of the response will be more predictable in line with what you‚Äôve already written.

Pushing the slider all the way to Most gives Sudowrite a whole bunch of creative agency. Consider this a promotion for your junior writing partner; Sudowrite now has creative license to take your story down much more unexpected paths.

By default the slider is right in the middle. But where should you keep it? That‚Äôs up to you and what you need from Sudowrite for your story.

### Number of Cards

This drop down controls how many cards (or variants) are generated in your History column (on the right side) when you use a Write feature. You can set this anywhere from 1 to 6.

### Length of Cards

This drop down allows you to set a rough approximation of how long each individual card should be in a response. It ranges from ~50 words all the way up to ~1,500 words. There‚Äôs also a Max Words setting, which depends on the Prose Mode selected but could be up to 2,000 words. Each time Sudowrite generates a card, that‚Äôs a new pass at the request on the part of the AI, so the results will vary.

### Key Details

This section informs Write when it is in either Auto or Guided mode. It‚Äôs also worth noting Key Details is a setting for the entire Project (üóÇÔ∏è) rather than a Document (üìÑ) level setting. Key Details **does not** affect Canvas or Story Bible.

As you move between documents in your Project, make sure to either turn off Key Details or change the information it contains if its contents do not apply to the scene you‚Äôre working on.

Great things to put into Key Details:

-   how you want the prose written such as ‚Äúromantic‚Äù or ‚Äúforeboding‚Äù
    
-   what heat level or intensity you want romance or violence scenes to be written
    
-   what genre are you writing in, keep in mind you might need to define newer genres or abbreviations to Sudowrite like ‚Äúreverse harem‚Äù or ‚Äúmpreg‚Äù
    
-   character names and brief details that are needed for the story, such as ‚ÄúLogan‚Äôs right arm was broken ten minutes before this scene happens, he is feeling incredible pain‚Äù
    
-   setting details that you want worked in such as ‚Äúgrand English country estate‚Äù
    
-   _and anything else you can think of, write in here like you would give notes to a junior writing partner!_
    

### What Does the Write Button See?

Write doesn‚Äôt just see the words just before your cursor anymore. Both Auto and Guided Write look at a bunch of context from your project, and take that all into consideration before writing for you Things that Write may reference as input context includes:

-   **Preceding Text**: Up to 20,000 words from the current document before the cursor
    
-   **Previous Document Text**: Up to 20,000 words total from linked chapters
    
    -   Up to 25 documents in the chain, working backwards from the current doc
        
-   **Key Details**: Anything you‚Äôve written in the Key Details field
    
-   **POV**: Point of view setting (e.g., "1st person", "3rd person close", "3rd person omniscient", etc.). This may included the Character name for POV perspective.
    
-   **Tense**: Tense setting (e.g., past tense, present tense)
    
-   **\[Guided Only\] Guidance**: User-provided plot point or writing direction
    
-   Story Bible (when turned on), including:
    
    -   **Genre**: Selected genre(s) for the story
        
    -   **Style**: Writing style guidelines
        
    -   **Synopsis**: Story synopsis
        
    -   **Outline Chapter Summary**: Details on the linked chapter from the Outline
